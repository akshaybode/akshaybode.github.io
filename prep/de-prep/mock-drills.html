Mock Interview Drills — Data Engineer

Four timed rounds that mirror a real mid-level DE loop (Snowflake + dbt + Airflow). Do them out loud, or type answers in chat and I'll grade them (see "Live mock").

How to use

  • Set a timer. Speak answers aloud as if to an interviewer.
  • Don't peek at the cheat sheets during the round; review after.
  • Score yourself with the rubric at the bottom, or paste answers in chat for grading.

Round 1 — Screening / Rapid Fire (30 min)

Goal: breadth and fundamentals, clear and quick. ~2–3 min each.

  1. Walk me through your background and why data engineering. (2 min)
  2. GROUP BY vs PARTITION BY — with an example.
  3. Write SQL: 2nd highest salary per department.
  4. Transformation vs action in Spark — give examples.
  5. repartition vs coalesce.
  6. ETL vs ELT — which do you prefer today and why?
  7. Star vs snowflake schema.
  8. What's a fact table grain? Give one for telecom billing.
  9. Parquet vs CSV — why does it matter?
  10. What does idempotent mean for a pipeline, and how do you achieve it?
  11. What is a dbt ref()?
  12. What is an Airflow DAG?

Round 2 — SQL + Python/PySpark Coding (45 min)

Goal: hands-on. Expect a shared editor; talk through your approach.

SQL (do these for real): 1. Given invoices(customer_id, invoice_date, amount), return each customer's running total of amount ordered by date. 2. Deduplicate raw_customers keeping the most recent row per customer_id. 3. Sessionize events(user_id, event_ts) with a 30-minute inactivity gap. 4. Find customers whose revenue grew vs the previous month (YoY/MoM).

PySpark (write it): 5. Read a Parquet folder, filter amount > 0, add a month column, aggregate total amount per customer per month, write partitioned by month. 6. You join a 1B-row fact to a 10K-row dimension and it's slow. What do you do? - Follow-up: explain shuffle vs broadcast join. 7. A job runs fine on small data but OOMs / is slow at scale. How do you debug it? - Follow-up: skew, partition sizing, caching, AQE.

Round 3 — Data Modeling + Warehouse Deep Dive (45 min)

Goal: depth and trade-offs. Expect "why?" follow-ups.

  1. Design a dimensional model for telecom billing analytics. State the grain, facts, and dimensions.
  2. A customer changes their plan — how do you preserve historical revenue accuracy? (Drive to SCD Type 2; describe the columns and the update logic.)
  3. Explain Snowflake's architecture and why storage/compute separation matters.
  4. What are micro-partitions and how does pruning work? When add a clustering key?
  5. How does Snowflake caching work (3 layers)?
  6. How would you load 50M rows/day into Snowflake and keep it idempotent?
  7. Walk me through how you'd build this transform layer in dbt (models, tests, snapshots, incremental).
  8. How do you control Snowflake cost on this workload?

Round 4 — Pipeline System Design + Project + Behavioral (60 min)

Goal: end-to-end thinking + your story.

System design (use the 09 framework): 1. Design an end-to-end pipeline: source billing DB → analytics in Snowflake, daily freshness, BI + finance consumers. Cover ingestion, storage, transform, serving, orchestration, quality, idempotency, cost, and monitoring. - Follow-ups: What if they now need near-real-time? How do you handle a source schema change? How do you reconcile counts vs source? Where's the PII risk? 2. How would you migrate your current Amdocs Oracle ETL to a cloud lakehouse?

Project deep-dive: 3. Walk me through the pipeline you built (project/README.md). Why each tool? What was the hardest part? What would you do differently at 100x scale?

Behavioral (STAR — see 10): 4. Tell me about your hardest technical problem. 5. A time you optimized something slow (with numbers). 6. Why are you looking to move? (Keep it professional.) 7. Your questions for us.

Rapid-fire flashcard topics (5-min blitzes)

Pick one and answer every term fast: window functions · SCD types · shuffle/broadcast · micro-partitions · dbt materializations · Airflow catchup/backfill · ETL vs ELT · Parquet · idempotency · batch vs streaming.

Self-grading rubric (1–5 per answer)

  • 5 — Correct, concise, with a trade-off or real example. Sounds experienced.
  • 4 — Correct and clear, minor gap.
  • 3 — Mostly right, vague or missing the "why."
  • 2 — Partial; key misconception.
  • 1 — Incorrect or blank.

Target before interviewing: avg ≥ 4 on Rounds 1–2, ≥ 3.5 on Rounds 3–4.
