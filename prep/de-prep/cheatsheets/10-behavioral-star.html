10 — Behavioral & Your Career-Switch Story (STAR)

The story round decides whether your switch reads as "experienced engineer moving into DE" (strong) or "beginner with no DE experience" (weak). Frame it as the former — honestly. Your Amdocs telecom work IS data engineering-adjacent.

STAR format

  • Situation (context, 1 sentence) → Task (your goal) → Action (what you did, the bulk) → Result (impact, ideally quantified). Keep to ~90 seconds.

Your positioning statement (the "tell me about yourself")

"I'm a software engineer with ~5 years building and maintaining data-heavy systems in the telecom billing/ordering domain at Amdocs — complex Oracle data models, SQL/PL-SQL, batch processing over high-volume billing data, and extract/ETL jobs. That work is essentially data engineering, and I've been deliberately deepening the modern stack — PySpark, dimensional modeling, Snowflake, dbt, and Airflow — and built an end-to-end pipeline to apply it. I'm looking to move into a dedicated Data Engineering role where data is the product."

Reframe Amdocs work into DE language (all true)

What you did How to say it
Amdocs billing/ordering modules "ETL/batch pipelines over high-volume telecom billing & ordering data"
Worked with Oracle data models (1,600+ tables) "Data modeling and SQL across large normalized schemas"
PL/SQL stored procedures, extract tools "Built data extraction/transformation logic; optimized long-running SQL"
Debugging ORA errors, query tuning "Performance tuning and query optimization on large datasets"
Batch job manager / scheduled jobs "Scheduled, monitored, and recovered batch data workflows"

How to handle the experience gap honestly

  • Don't claim production Snowflake/dbt/Airflow you haven't run. Instead:

    "I haven't run dbt in production yet, but I built [project] with it — here's how I'd approach incremental models and tests in your environment."

  • Lead with transferable depth (SQL, modeling, debugging at scale) + your project as proof.

Must-prep STAR stories (write 5–6, reuse across questions)

  1. Hardest technical problem — a nasty data/SQL/performance bug at Amdocs.
  2. Performance optimization — a slow query/batch job you made fast (numbers!).
  3. Handling a production incident / data issue — found root cause, fixed, prevented.
  4. Learning something fast — exactly this DE switch / a new module under deadline.
  5. Working with stakeholders / ambiguity — translated a business need into a data deliverable.
  6. A project you're proud of — your end-to-end pipeline (project/README.md).

Common behavioral questions

  • Why do you want to move into data engineering? (Genuine answer: you already work with data at scale and want to do it with the modern stack, as your core role.)
  • Why are you leaving / open to leaving? (Neutral & professional: "exploring growth in a dedicated DE role; my company is restructuring." Never bad-mouth.)
  • Tell me about a conflict / a mistake / a time you missed a deadline.
  • How do you prioritize when everything is urgent?
  • Where do you see yourself / why this company?

On the layoff (if it comes up)

  • Keep it factual and forward-looking. "There's a restructuring at my company, so I'm proactively pursuing the data engineering direction I'd been building toward anyway."
  • No bitterness, no over-sharing. Pivot to enthusiasm for the role.

Questions to ask them (shows seniority)

  • What does the data stack and pipeline look like today? Batch or streaming?
  • Biggest data-quality or scaling challenge the team faces right now?
  • How is the DE team structured, and how do DE/analytics/ML collaborate?
  • What would success in this role look like in the first 6 months?

Delivery tips

  • Quantify results (rows, %, hours saved, latency). Use "I" not "we" for your actions.
  • Pause, structure, answer. It's fine to say "let me think for a second."
  • Honesty + clear reasoning beats buzzword bingo every time.
