1. What Is Data Architecture?

From Fundamentals of Data Engineering: "the design of systems to support the evolving data needs of an enterprise, achieved by flexible and reversible decisions reached through careful evaluation of trade-offs."

The building analogy: Before constructing a 12-floor building you draw a blueprint (foundation, floor plans, elevators, stairs, restrooms). For a data system the blueprint covers storage, software, data flow, interfaces, transformations, staging areas, warehouses, reporting.
  • Flexible & reversible: any component should be easy to swap if a decision turns out wrong.
  • Trade-offs: choose technologies based on your actual requirements, not hype.

2. Two Halves: Operational vs Technical

🎯 Operational Architecture — the "WHY"

Focus on business goals & requirements. Define what the business needs before touching any technology.

Example business questions

  • What's the impact of product category XYZ?
  • Why is there a delay in product shipping?
  • How do we manage data quality from third-party vendors?

⚙️ Technical Architecture — the "HOW"

Focus on the technology to ingest, store, transform data. Think about scale.

Example technical questions

  • How do we ingest and transform data?
  • What happens during a sudden order spike (festival)?
  • How do we scale the system?
Key: Technology is easy to build once you know what the business needs. If you don't define the business need first, you'll be stuck designing forever.

3. Operational Principles (Defining Business Goals)

  • Start with the end in mind — always begin from the business problem you're solving.
  • Iterate and evolve — business changes every ~6 months (new products, new priorities); the architecture must adapt quickly.
  • Focus on impact — every solution must have a clear line of sight to a measurable business outcome (customer satisfaction, streamlined ops, better decisions).

4. Technical Principles (Building Blocks)

  • Simplicity is key — simpler systems are easier to maintain, scale, and debug.
  • Right tool for the job — no one-size-fits-all. Structured data → warehouse; few million rows → maybe just Athena ad-hoc queries instead of expensive Snowflake.
  • Build for scale & flexibility — plan for growth (e.g., Python now, Apache Spark cluster later). Start small, scale as you grow.
  • Embed automation — automate pipelines & monitoring; get email/Slack alerts on errors instead of checking manually.
  • Prioritize security & governance — encrypt data, secure the network, prevent data leakage.
Trade-offs over hype: A simple Python script that solves the problem beats a fancy Snowflake + Spark stack that doesn't add value. Open-source or enterprise — whatever solves the business problem at the right cost wins.

5. Worked Example — E-commerce Platform

Step 1: Define business goals (operational)

GoalWhat it means
Improve customer experienceBetter site navigation, personalized recommendations, enhanced customer service.
Operational efficiencyStreamline inventory, order processing, shipping → reduce cost & delivery time.
Marketing insightsUnderstand customer behavior → improve product placement & sales.
Vendor managementBetter availability, pricing strategy, quality control across vendors.
Compliance & securityFollow regulations (e.g., mask / don't store raw credit-card info).

Step 2: Design technical layers

LayerPurposeExample tools
Data ingestionCollect from site interactions, server logs, vendor systems, inventory, supportApache Kafka (real-time streaming)
StorageStore collected data for access & analysisS3 (unstructured/lake), Snowflake / BigQuery / Redshift (structured)
Processing & transformationClean, validate, structure raw dataApache Spark (large data) or Python (smaller data)
Analysis & businessDashboards & ML for predictionsTableau, Power BI; TensorFlow, PyTorch
Security & complianceMeet GDPR/CCPA, encrypt/mask PIIEncryption, data masking
Integration & APIMove data between systems & vendorsCustom APIs
Choosing Snowflake vs Redshift? "It depends." Already on AWS → Redshift integrates well. Too expensive → Snowflake or open source. Always do a small PoC (proof of concept) with your real data size & frequency, then pick what works best.
